Methodology

Five levers of geometric selectivity.

Selectivity rests on a small number of parameters that map cleanly to bird anatomy and behavior. Understanding what each one does makes it easy to design for species not listed here.

i

Port diameter

Tracks beak-plus-head width. Set just below the head width of the smallest unwanted species — exclusion threshold matters more than the target.

ii

Perch length

Short perches (10–18 mm) favor small clingy birds; long perches (30+ mm) let larger birds settle their full body weight. Set to 0 to remove perches entirely.

iii

Perch angle

The most underused lever. Downward angles (−10° to −20°) favor inverted feeders and physically exclude grackles, starlings, and most blackbirds.

iv

Roof overhang

Keeps seed dry and blocks aerial approach from larger hover-capable birds. Aim for roughly 1.5× the perch projection.

v

Port height from base

Where ports sit vertically on the cylinder. Higher placement (50 mm+) suits clinging species that approach from above; lower placement (35 mm) suits perched feeders reaching in from the side.

Note — Geometric exclusion is statistical, not absolute. Juveniles are smaller than adults of the same species and may pass through openings rated for adults. Treat these values as starting points, observe what actually visits, and iterate.

REGION 03 / NORTH AMERICA

Pacific Northwest

Target
Chestnut-backed chickadee, red-breasted nuthatch, pine siskin, dark-eyed junco, Anna's hummingbird (nectar variant).
Excluding
House sparrow, European starling, brown-headed cowbird.

This region's signature design move is no perches at all — chestnut-backed chickadees and red-breasted nuthatches prefer to cling and feed inverted, and removing perches entirely is one of the cleanest exclusion mechanisms for sparrows and starlings, which need a perching surface. Ports are also smaller and raised higher on the cylinder to suit clinging approach.

REGION 04 / OCEANIA

Australian Honeyeaters

Target
New Holland honeyeater, eastern spinebill, white-plumed honeyeater, brown honeyeater, silvereye.
Excluding
Common myna, house sparrow, European starling. (Noisy miner: native but aggressive — your call.)

Honeyeaters have longer slender beaks adapted for nectar feeding, which calls for wider ports than you'd use elsewhere. The current preset uses a 38 mm round port — large enough for a New Holland honeyeater's beak-and-head reach, with longer perches to support the bird's larger body.

Reverse reference

Exclusion thresholds.

If you know what you want to exclude, work backward from these thresholds.

Caveat — Juveniles are smaller than adults of the same species. A port rated to exclude adult starlings may admit juveniles for the few weeks they're at fledgling size. This is not a design flaw — it's a property of geometric exclusion that any honest birder will recognize.
Generalize the method

Designing for a species not listed.

The methodology generalizes. To add a new target species, work through the seven steps below — and if your preset works in your area, contribute it back.

Look up body mass, body length, and beak length in Cornell Birds of the World, your regional ornithological handbook, or a peer-reviewed source. Cross-check at least one source.

Estimate head width as roughly 60–70% of body width at the shoulders, or use direct measurements where available.

Set port diameter to head width + 4–6 mm clearance — unless a smaller unwanted species shares your area, in which case set it just below that species' head width.

Set perch length to roughly 1.0–1.3× the foot-to-shoulder distance — long enough to grip, short enough that body weight doesn't fully settle.

Set perch angle based on feeding posture: 0° for upright feeders, −10° to −15° for inverted-capable species, vertical or no perch for clingers.

Set roof overhang to roughly 1.5× perch projection, or larger in high-rainfall regions.

Print, install, observe for two weeks, iterate. If the preset works, fork the KCL file and open a pull request.

About this guide

A reference companion to the parametric bird feeder KCL model. Values here are starting points based on published body measurements and field-tested cage-mesh selectivity research — not field-validated calibrations. Tune based on observed visitors in your yard.
